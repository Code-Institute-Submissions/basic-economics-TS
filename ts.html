A Citizen's Guide to the Economy

 

Thomas Sowell

Economist and Social Theorist

Thomas Sowell has studied and taught economics and intellectual history and social policies at institutions that include Cornell, Brandeis, UCLA and Amherst. Now a senior fellow at the Hoover institution, Dr. Sowell has published over a dozen of books. Dr. Sowell grew up in Harlem, and dropped out of high school at 16, ended up received an undergraduate degree from Harvard, a master’s degree from Colombia and his doctorate degree from the university of Chicago.

Growing up in Harlem and getting his first job delivering telegrams in Manhattan, Dr. Sowell really got exposed to the living conditions of the very rich and the poor. When going home from work he would sometimes splurge and take the Fifth Avenue bus home, which would drive by the very glamour’s part of the city and when he would eventually arrive at home he would wonder: why is this, why the huge disparity? At the time there was no other explanation around other than Marxism, the people who were rich had gotten rich by simply taking from the poor. This economic understanding was something Dr. Sowell carried with him into his early 20s, even when studding under what is considered some of the biggest defenders of free markets and founders of Chicago school of economics: Milton Friedman, George Stigler and Friedrich Hayek.

What eventually ended up changing Dr. Sowell’s mind was not the university of Chicago. It was his first job working at a professional capacity for the government. As a summer intern at the US department of labour he began to realise that: the government is not simply the personification of the general will; the government institutions have their own institutional interests. One of these involved the minimum wage law, which he was a big supporter of, but he also knew that there was an argument that minimum wage law simply priced low wage workers out of a job. Dr. Sowell’s first assignment dealt with minimum wage laws within the sugar industry in Puerto Rico and as he looked at the numbers he would see: as the minimum wage went up the number of jobs would go down. But there were two explanations: number one was the one of the economists that clamed people were being priced out of work. The other theory was that there were hurricanes that had come through Puerto Rico during the sugar harvesting and it destroyed a lot of the crop, therefor you wouldn’t hire as many workers.

In his time at the university of Chicago he had been taught that if there are two different theories there should be some imperial evidence in principle that could distinguish what would happen under one theory and what would happen under the other. He wrestled with that during the summer and one morning he came in and said: “I got it! What we need are data on the amount of sugar canes standing in the fields before the hurricane struck”. He waited for the applause and congratulations but instead got looks of chock, like: this guy has stumbled on top of something that is going to ruin us all!

He realised that his concern was if this law was beneficial or not beneficial to low income people, they were concerned because this law was providing a third of the income of the US department of labour. And once he started to see that the government agencies have their own self-interest, quite aside from whatever they are theoretically supposed to be doing, he went on to find out how the world really works, in terms of economic markets.
